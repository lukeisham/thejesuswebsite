The Crucifixion of Jesus

Matthew 27:32-66 · Mark 15:21-47 · Luke 23:26-56 · John 19:17-42
Death & Resurrection Judea 30-33 CE Roman Custom

Description

The crucifixion of Jesus Christ stands as one of the most historically significant events in human history. This section presents the available historical evidence from both biblical and extra-biblical sources regarding the circumstances, participants, and theological implications of this central Christian event.

The Roman custom of crucifixion was an ancient method of capital punishment reserved for the most serious criminals and enemies of the state. Archaeological and historical evidence confirms its widespread use throughout the Roman Empire during the first century CE.

Timeline Context

The crucifixion occurred during the Roman governorship of Pontius Pilate (26-36 CE). The timing of this event places it squarely within the early first century CE, during the reign of Tiberius Caesar and the high priesthood of Caiaphas in Jerusalem.

Historical Setting

Jerusalem under Roman occupation experienced frequent tensions between the local Jewish authority and Roman rulers. The movement led by Jesus of Nazareth had attracted significant attention from both religious and political authorities.

Key Historical Detail

Multiple independent historical sources attest to the general fact of Jesus' crucifixion under Pontius Pilate. This marks one of the most solidly established facts about Jesus in historical scholarship.

Primary Sources

Gospel Accounts

The four canonical gospels provide the most detailed accounts of the crucifixion narrative. While they share a common core narrative, they differ in certain details and emphasis:

Matthew 27:35-44 And when they came to the place called The Skull, they crucified him there with the criminals, one on his right and one on his left. And Jesus said, "Father, forgive them, for they know not what they do." And they cast lots to divide his garments.

Extra-Biblical Sources

Historical documentation beyond the Christian scriptures provides crucial corroboration for key facts about the crucifixion:

Tacitus, the Roman historian writing in the early second century, mentioned the execution of Jesus under Pontius Pilate, providing independent confirmation of the crucifixion event by a non-Christian source.

Tacitus, Annals 15.44 Christus, from whom the name had its origin, suffered the extreme penalty during the reign of Tiberius at the hands of one of our procurators, Pontius Pilate...

Physical Evidence

Archaeological investigations have uncovered significant evidence regarding Roman crucifixion practices. The remains of Yohanan ben Hagkol, discovered in a Jerusalem ossuary in 1968, provide the only known archaeological evidence of an actual crucified individual.

Analysis of these remains revealed a nail through the heel bone, consistent with crucifixion methodology, and confirmed the physical mechanism of this execution method.
