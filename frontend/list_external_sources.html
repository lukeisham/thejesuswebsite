List of external sources

A detailed list of 'external' or 'hostile' sources from 33 AD to 313 AD. (Emperor Constantine issued the Edict of Milan in 313 AD, making Christianity the official religion of the Roman Empire.)

0 – 313 AD (External References)

  • Flavius Josephus Antiquities 18 & 20 — Jewish historian who records the execution of James, "the brother of Jesus, who was called Christ," and the "Testimonium Flavianum" regarding Jesus’ life and crucifixion. (c. 93 AD)
  • Cornelius Tacitus Annals 15.44 — Roman historian who confirms "Christus" was executed by the procurator Pontius Pilate during the reign of Tiberius. (c. 116 AD)
  • Pliny the Younger Epistulae 10.96 — Roman governor writing to Emperor Trajan, describing Christians meeting before dawn to sing hymns to Christ "as to a god." (c. 112 AD)
  • Suetonius Life of Claudius 25.4 — Roman biographer who mentions the expulsion of Jews from Rome due to disturbances instigated by "Chrestus" (Christ). (c. 121 AD)
  • Mara Bar-Serapion Syriac Letter — A Stoic philosopher who reflects on the execution of the "Wise King" of the Jews and the subsequent ruin of the Jewish kingdom. (Late 1st – Early 2nd Century AD)
  • Lucian of Samosata The Passing of Peregrinus — Greek satirist who references the "distinguished personage" crucified in Palestine for introducing new religious rites. (c. 165 AD)
  • Thallus & Phlegon Fragmentary (via Africanus) — Ancient chroniclers whose (now lost) works reportedly attempted to provide a naturalistic explanation for the darkness during the crucifixion. (c. 55–140 AD)
  • The Babylonian Talmud Sanhedrin 43a — Early Jewish tradition (Tannaitic period) recording that "Yeshu" was hanged on the eve of Passover for practicing sorcery and leading Israel astray. (Pre-200 AD traditions)
  • Celsus The True Word (via Origen) — A Platonist philosopher who wrote the first comprehensive intellectual attack on Christianity. He confirms Jesus' reputation for miracles (which he attributed to sorcery learned in Egypt) and his low-born status. (c. 175–180 AD)
  • Emperor Hadrian Letter to Minucius Fundanus — A Roman imperial decree establishing legal procedures for trying Christians, emphasizing that they should only be punished for actual illegal acts rather than just the "name" of Christian. (c. 125 AD)
  • Galen De Pulsuum Differentiis — The famous Roman physician and philosopher who mentions the "followers of Moses and Christ" and their unyielding faith/discipline, comparing their schools of thought to medical sects. (c. 150–200 AD)
  • Marcus Aurelius Meditations 11.3 — The Roman Emperor/Stoic philosopher who notes the "sheer obstinacy" of Christians in their readiness to die for their faith. (c. 167 AD)
  • Alexamenos Graffito Palatine Hill Inscription — While not a text, this "external" archaeological reference is a carved mockery found in Rome showing a man worshipping a crucified figure with a donkey's head, captioned "Alexamenos worships [his] God." (c. 200 AD)
  • Porphyry of Tyre Against the Christians — A Neoplatonist who provided a sophisticated critique of the Gospels, noting contradictions in the genealogies and the trial of Jesus, confirming the texts were well-established by his time. (c. 270 AD)
