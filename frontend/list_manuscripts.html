List of Manuscripts

A detailed list of Gospel manuscript fragments from 33 AD to 313 AD, and then a selection of significant Gospel manuscripts from 314 AD to 500 AD. (Emperor Constantine issued the Edict of Milan in 313 AD, making Christianity the official religion of the Roman Empire.) The manuscript name is in bold, followed by the manuscript number (egGA: P52) and the location (egFondation Bodmer), and then a brief description. Notably there are other New Testament fragments from second and third centuries that are not included in this list.

33 – 313 AD

  • P52 GA: P52 (Rylands Library) — The oldest widely accepted fragment of the New Testament (John 18). (~125 – 150 AD)
  • P66 GA: P66 (Fondation Bodmer) — A near-complete codex of the Gospel of John. (~150 – 175 AD)
  • P75 GA: P75 (Fondation Bodmer) — Extensive remains of Luke and John. (~175 – 225 AD)
  • P104 GA: P104 (Sackler Library) — An exceptionally early fragment of Matthew 21. (~150 – 200 AD)
  • P90 GA: P90 (Sackler Library) — A small fragment of John 18; a second very early witness to the Fourth Gospel. (~150 – 175 AD)
  • P64/P67 GA: P64 (Magdalen College) — Three fragments of Matthew 26; notable for early "Nomina Sacra." (~175 – 200 AD)
  • P45 GA: P45 (Chester Beatty Library) — Fragments of all four Gospels and Acts. (~250 AD)
  • Oxyrhynchus Papyri GA: Various (Oxyrhynchus Online) — Numerous fragments including Matthew and various non-canonical works. (2nd – 4th Century)
  • P1 GA: P1 (Univ. of Pennsylvania) — An early papyrus containing the beginning of Matthew 1. (~250 AD)
  • Dura-Europos GA: 0212 (Yale Beinecke Library) — A fragment containing a portion of Tatian’s Diatessaron, a harmony of the four Gospels (Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John). (< 256 AD)
  • P37 GA: P37 (Univ. of Michigan) — A well-preserved fragment of Matthew 26 in a formal hand. (~250 – 300 AD)
  • P137 GA: P137 (Sackler Library) — The earliest known fragment of the Gospel of Mark (Mark 1). (~150 – 250 AD)
  • P77 GA: P77 (Sackler Library) — An early witness to Matthew 23, likely paired originally with P103. (~200 AD)
  • P4 GA: P4 (Bibliothèque nationale de France) — One of the oldest fragments of Luke (175–200 AD)
  • P53 (University of Michigan) — Portions of Matthew 26, specifically the trial before Caiaphas. (~250 AD)
  • P70 (Sackler Library) — Multiple fragments from Matthew 2, 3, 11, and 24. (~250–300 AD)
  • P101 (Sackler Library) — Early fragments of Matthew 3 and 4. (~200–250 AD)
  • P103 (Sackler Library) — Matthew 23; physically matches P77 to form a single witness. (~200 AD)
  • P110 (Sackler Library) — Portions of Matthew 10. (~250–300 AD)
  • P129 (Museum of the Bible) — Fragmentary portions of Mark 7, 8, and 9. (~200–250 AD)
    • P7 (Vernon Bartlet Collection) — A small fragment of Luke 4. (~250–300 AD)
  • P42 (Austrian National Library) — Luke 1 and 2; a rare early bilingual (Greek/Sahidic) fragment. (~175–225 AD)
  • P69 (Sackler Library) — A witness to Luke 22. (~250 AD)
  • P82 (National Library of France) — Portions of Luke 7. (~250–300 AD)
    • P5 (British Library) — Significant fragments of John 1, 16, and 20. (~250 AD)
  • P22 (Glasgow University Library) — Portions of John 15 and 16. (~250 AD)
  • P28 (Palau-Ribes Collection) — A small fragment of John 6. (~250 AD)
  • P39 (Papyri of the Vatican) — A fragment of John 8. (~250 AD)
  • P80 (Palau-Ribes Collection) — A witness to John 3. (~250 AD)
  • P95 (Laurentian Library) — A fragment of John 5. (~200–250 AD)
  • P106 (Sackler Library) — Portions of John 1. (~250 AD)
  • P108 (Sackler Library) — Fragments of John 17 and 18. (~175–225 AD)
  • P109 (Sackler Library) — A fragment of John 21. (~175–225 AD)

314 – 500 AD

  • Codex Sinaiticus GA: א (01) (British Library) — One of the most important complete manuscripts of the Bible.
  • Codex Vaticanus GA: B (03) (Vatican Library) — Major uncial manuscript containing most of the Old and New Testaments.
  • Codex Vercellensis GA: it-a (Biblioteca Capitolare) — The most important manuscript of the Old Latin version of the Gospels.
  • P87 GA: P87 (Univ. of Cologne) — The oldest known fragment of the Epistle to Philemon.
