Early Church Fathers (33 – 325 AD)

In addition to the New Testamen and its authors, the early church fathers prior to the Council of Nicaea in 325AD, repsent another way of verifying the historical nature of Jesus.

33 – 150 AD (The Apostolic Fathers)

  • Clement of Rome 1 Clement — Fourth Bishop of Rome. His letter to Corinth (c. 96 AD) is one of the earliest Christian documents outside the NT.
  • Ignatius of Antioch The Seven Letters — Writing while en route to his martyrdom in Rome, he provides crucial early testimony on the church's structure and the nature of Christ. (d. c. 108 AD)
  • Polycarp of Smyrna Epistle to the Philippians — A student of the Apostle John. His life serves as a bridge between the apostolic age and the 2nd-century apologists. (c. 69–155 AD)
  • Papias of Hierapolis Fragments — An early bishop who preserved oral traditions regarding the origins of the Gospels of Mark and Matthew. (c. 60–130 AD)

150 – 250 AD (The Early Apologists)

  • Justin Martyr Apologies / Dialogue with Trypho — A converted philosopher who defended Christianity to the Roman Senate and Emperor Antoninus Pius. (c. 100–165 AD)
  • Irenaeus of Lyons Adversus Haereses — A vital link to the East (disciple of Polycarp) who served in the West. He was the first to formalize the theology of the four Gospels. (c. 130–202 AD)
  • Tertullian of Carthage Apologeticus — The first major Christian author to write in Latin; he coined many theological terms still used today, including "Trinity." (c. 155–220 AD)
  • Hippolytus of Rome Refutation of All Heresies — A rigorous defender of tradition in Rome who documented early liturgical practices and historical chronology. (c. 170–235 AD)
  • Origen of Alexandria De Principiis — Perhaps the greatest scholar of the early church, he produced the first systematic theology and massive biblical commentaries. (c. 184–253 AD)

250 – 325 AD (The Final Ante-Nicene Generation)

  • Cyprian of Carthage Unity of the Church — Bishop during the Decian persecution. He focused heavily on the nature of the church and the validity of baptism. (c. 210–258 AD)
  • Lactantius Divinae Institutiones — An advisor to Constantine who wrote to explain Christianity to educated Romans during the transition to a Christian empire. (c. 250–325 AD)
  • Eusebius of Caesarea Ecclesiastical History — The "Father of Church History." Though he lived past 325, his primary work was the collection and preservation of pre-325 documents and records. (c. 260–339 AD)
  • Alexander of Alexandria Epistles on Arianism — The bishop who initially opposed Arius, leading directly to the Council of Nicaea. (d. 326 AD)
